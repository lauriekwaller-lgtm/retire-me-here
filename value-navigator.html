The ValueNavigator

For retirees who want to know exactly what their income buys — low cost of living, favorable taxes, and housing that doesn't swallow the budget. This guide ranks our database of 100 cities by budget and tax friendliness, scored separately.

💰 100 Cities Scored
🏠 Budget + Tax
🗓 May 2026
What we looked for: two things, scored separately. Budget is what a comfortable retirement realistically costs each month — housing, healthcare, utilities, food, lifestyle — expressed as a 1–5 range and a monthly estimate. Tax friendliness is scored on its own, because a city can have cheap housing but punishing taxes on retirement income, or the reverse. The real signal is the combination. Every dollar figure, budget range, and tax score here comes straight from our city database, not from listing-site estimates.

Before You Scroll

Four things to know about retirement value.

1
Budget and taxes are different questions. A city can be cheap to live in and still tax your retirement income hard, or be pricey but tax-free. Chattanooga and Casper score the maximum on both — low monthly cost and zero state income tax. St. Paul has genuinely affordable housing but one of the heaviest tax loads in the database. Always read the two together.
2
The deepest value clusters in the South and Mountain West. Tennessee, Texas, and Wyoming pair low costs with no state income tax. Chattanooga, Johnson City, Corpus Christi, Fort Worth, and Casper all land in the two cheapest budget ranges with strong tax scores. The Southeast's smaller cities — Greenville, Knoxville, Fayetteville — deliver similar value at modest housing prices.
3
The Northeast isn't automatically expensive. Pittsburgh ($425K–$700K) and Philadelphia ($475K–$975K) are the surprises — major-metro healthcare and culture at prices that undercut much of the Sun Belt. Pennsylvania's flat income tax is among the lowest, and Social Security is exempt. The catch is local wage and property taxes, so the picture is good rather than spectacular.
4
California, New York, and the resort towns carry a double premium. Carlsbad, Santa Barbara, Carmel, Napa, Palm Springs, and Saratoga Springs combine the highest budget ranges with the worst tax scores — premium housing prices and a large bite out of retirement income. The mountain-luxury tier (Jackson Hole, Park City, Vail, Sun Valley) is just as costly, though Wyoming's zero income tax softens Jackson Hole.

Spotlight 1

Best Pure Value — Top 10

The strongest combinations of low cost of living and favorable tax treatment in the database. These cities score highest on affordability and back it up with light or zero taxes on retirement income — the places where a modest income stretches the furthest.

1
ChattanoogaTN
★ Best Value in Database
Range 1 · $3,000–$4,000/mo Tax 10/10
Typical home value $314K. Among the most affordable cities in the Southeast, paired with no Tennessee income tax on any retirement income — Social Security, pension, and IRA withdrawals all untouched. Real healthcare and serious outdoor recreation on top. The cleanest budget-plus-tax combination we found.
2
CasperWY
Range 1 · $2,800–$3,800/mo Tax 10/10
Typical home value $308K. Wyoming levies no income tax of any kind — zero on Social Security, pension, and investment income. Combine that with a low monthly cost and Casper becomes the best value in the database for tax-motivated retirees willing to trade big-city amenities for plains-and-mountains quiet.
3
Corpus ChristiTX
Range 2 · $3,000–$4,000/mo Tax 9/10
Typical home value $226K — the lowest of any coastal city in the database. Gulf beach access at a price that undercuts every Florida beach town by a wide margin. No Texas income tax amplifies the advantage. Property taxes are the trade-off Texas always carries, but the entry price is hard to beat on the water.
4
Johnson CityTN
★ Most Affordable City in Database
Range 1 · $2,500–$3,200/mo Tax 8/10
Typical home value $294K, monthly cost from $2,500. The lowest monthly estimate of any city we scored — a genuinely comfortable retirement is possible on a modest fixed income here. No Tennessee income tax, Appalachian mountain access, and a small-city pace. Healthcare depth is the main thing to check against your own needs.
5
Sioux FallsSD
Range 2 · $3,000–$4,000/mo Tax 10/10
Typical home value $333K. South Dakota has no state income tax, and Sioux Falls anchors it with a surprisingly deep regional healthcare presence for its size. Low monthly costs and a stable small-metro economy. Winters are the real trade — but the financial case is among the strongest anywhere.
6
KnoxvilleTN
Range 2 · $3,200–$4,200/mo Tax 9/10
Typical home value $371K. No Tennessee income tax, Great Smoky Mountains 30 minutes away, and a university-town energy that keeps culture and healthcare strong. One of the best value propositions in a city with genuine depth rather than just a low price tag.
7
SpokaneWA
Range 2 · $3,200–$4,500/mo Tax 9/10
Typical home value $401K. Affordable Pacific Northwest — substantially cheaper than Seattle, Portland, or Bend for similar inland-outdoor access. Washington has no income tax on wages or retirement income. The most accessible way into the PNW lifestyle without the coastal price tag.
8
TucsonAZ
Range 2 · $3,200–$4,200/mo Tax 8/10
Typical home value $325K. Real university culture, Saguaro National Park at the edge of town, and Arizona's flat 2.5% income tax — one of the lowest rates among states that tax income at all. Far more affordable than Scottsdale or Sedona for the same desert-Southwest base.
9
Fort WorthTX
Range 2 · $3,000–$4,200/mo Tax 8/10
Typical home value $300K. A major Texas metro — full airport, healthcare, and cultural infrastructure — at a monthly cost most big cities can't touch. No Texas income tax. Property taxes run high, the standing Texas trade-off, but the overall value for a metro this size is strong.
10
FayettevilleAR
Range 1 · $2,800–$3,800/mo Tax 7/10
Typical home value $380K. The best-value college town in the database — University of Arkansas energy, Ozark trails, and a Razorback Greenway, all on a Range 1 budget. Arkansas taxes income modestly but exempts a meaningful share of retirement income. Outstanding value most lists overlook.
