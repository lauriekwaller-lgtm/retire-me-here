The UrbanWalkabout

Can you leave the car keys at home? This guide ranks our database of 100 cities by walkability — how much of daily life happens on foot, the quality of pedestrian and transit infrastructure, and whether the neighborhoods actually support a car-optional retirement.

Before You Scroll

Four things to know about walkability in retirement.

1
When you stop commuting, your neighborhood becomes your world. Walkability shapes daily activity, social connection, and how long you can stay independent without a car — in ways that climate and tax rankings never capture. Three cities top the database: Alexandria, Portland ME, and Provincetown.
2
The transit-plus-walkability combination is rare — and Alexandria has it. Five Metro stations, a free local bus, and Reagan National reachable by rail in 15 minutes. For retirees thinking about aging in place without driving, that pairing of a walkable core with real transit stands almost alone here.
3
A real historic downtown is the single best predictor. Savannah, Charleston, Annapolis, St. Augustine, Saratoga Springs — the most walkable cities share dense, intact cores built before cars dominated planning. "Does this city have a real historic downtown?" is the question to ask.
4
Watch the Walk Score trap, and most of Florida. A decent citywide average can hide a tiny walkable pocket in an otherwise car-dependent city — Scottsdale is the cautionary case. And Florida, built for cars, mostly scores low; Delray Beach, St. Augustine, and St. Petersburg are the real exceptions.

Tier 1

Car-Optional — Scores 8–9

Daily errands genuinely possible on foot. These are the cities where a car is a convenience, not a necessity, and where life happens at sidewalk level.

AlexandriaVA 9/10
★ Best Transit in the DatabaseFive Metro stations on the Blue and Yellow lines. Old Town scores 84–90 Walk Score; free DASH bus and King Street Trolley; Reagan National by Metro in 15 min. Car-optional living is genuinely achievable.
→
PortlandME 9/10
★ Most Walkable in DatabaseThe Old Port, Munjoy Hill, and West End are all walkable; the compact 68,000-person scale means daily life happens on foot. Among the best pedestrian cores in New England.
→
ProvincetownMA 9/10
A 3-mile peninsula village where almost nothing requires a car — Commercial Street runs the length of town, with dining, galleries, and beach all on foot. One of the most naturally walkable places in the country.
→
Ann ArborMI 8/10
State Street and downtown exceptionally walkable; the U-M campus, Kerrytown, and Main Street corridor all on foot. Solid AATA transit. One of the most walkable mid-sized college towns in the Midwest.
→
AnnapolisMD 8/10
The colonial street grid was built for walking — Main Street, City Dock, the Naval Academy, and the State House all on foot. Dense enough that waterfront living needs no car.
→
BoulderCO 8/10
Pearl Street pedestrian mall anchors a genuinely walkable core, with 300+ miles of trails and a deep cycling culture. The university and downtown blend into a car-light lifestyle.
→
Carmel-by-the-SeaCA 8/10
A storybook one-square-mile village with no street addresses — everything is on foot, from galleries to the beach. Walkability is the entire point of the place.
→
Delray BeachFL 8/10
Atlantic Avenue is genuinely walkable — one of Florida's best main streets, 90+ dining and shopping options plus beach access. Compact enough for car-optional downtown living.
→
New OrleansLA 8/10
The French Quarter, Garden District, and Marigny are dense and historic; the streetcar lines add real transit. One of the most walkable cities in the South.
→
PhiladelphiaPA 8/10
Walk Score 74, among the most walkable large US cities. Center City, Rittenhouse, Old City, and Fairmount all walkable, with two subway lines and regional rail. Car-optional living very achievable.
→
Santa BarbaraCA 8/10
State Street downtown and the waterfront are walkable, with a genuine Mediterranean pedestrian culture. Beyond downtown the city climbs into car-dependent hills.
→
Saratoga SpringsNY 8/10
Broadway and the Victorian downtown are genuinely walkable — Congress Park, the spa park, and the racetrack district all on foot. Compact and pleasurable.
→
St. PetersburgFL 8/10
A revitalized, dense downtown — the waterfront, museums, and Central Avenue arts district all walkable. The most pedestrian-friendly city on Florida's Gulf coast.
→

Tier 2

Walkable Core — Score 7

A real walkable core with a strong pedestrian culture, though the surrounding city still leans on a car. Live in the right neighborhood and most daily life happens on foot.

AshevilleNC 7/10
Downtown is dense and walkable — River Arts District, Pack Square, Lexington Avenue galleries all on foot. Smaller walkable zone than Savannah but packed with dining and arts.
→
BendOR 7/10
Downtown Bend and the Old Mill District are walkable, with the Deschutes River Trail exceptional for walking and cycling. Sprawl beyond, but a strong active core.
→
BurlingtonVT 7/10
The Church Street Marketplace pedestrian mall anchors a compact, walkable downtown between Lake Champlain and the university. Genuinely car-light for its size.
→
CharlestonSC 7/10
The historic peninsula is one of the most beautiful walkable cores in the South — King Street, Rainbow Row, the Battery all on foot. Beyond the peninsula needs a car.
→
CharlottesvilleVA 7/10
The Downtown Mall — eight car-free blocks — is one of the longest pedestrian malls in the US, packed with dining, bookstores, and the Paramount. UVA Corner also walkable.
→
EugeneOR 7/10
Downtown and the Whiteaker are walkable, with the Ruth Bascom Riverbank Trail among the finest urban trail networks in the Pacific Northwest.
→
FairhopeAL 7/10
One of the best small-town pedestrian experiences in the South — the bluff-top downtown, shops, and Eastern Shore waterfront all on foot, on intentionally tree-lined streets.
→
GreenvilleSC 7/10
Main Street and the West End are genuinely walkable — Falls Park on the Reedy and the 22-mile Swamp Rabbit Trail anchor one of the best small-city downtowns in the Southeast.
→
Iowa CityIA 7/10
A compact UNESCO City of Literature — the pedestrian mall, downtown, and university blend into a walkable core with strong transit for its size.
→
Jackson HoleWY 7/10
⚡ Walkable Core OnlyDowntown Jackson — Town Square, galleries, restaurants — is walkable, with free START Bus service. Beyond the compact center, the valley is entirely car-dependent.
→
SavannahGA 7/10
★ Best Walkable Design in the SouthThe Historic District's 22 squares were designed for pedestrians — Broughton Street, River Street, and Forsyth Park all on foot. One of America's great pedestrian-first city plans.
→
Silver SpringMD 7/10
Downtown is genuinely walkable around the Metro Red Line station — Ellsworth Drive dining, the AFI Silver, the Fillmore. Purple Line arriving 2027 adds a second transit corridor.
→
St. AugustineFL 7/10
The historic district predates cars entirely — St. George Street, the Castillo, and the bayfront all on foot. A colonial grid unlike any other Florida city.
→
St. PaulMN 7/10
Grand and Selby Avenues are walkable, with light rail to Minneapolis and a winter skyway system. Strong walkability, though winter limits outdoor walking several months a year.
→

Tier 3

Walkable Pockets — Scores 5–6

A walkable pocket or two inside an otherwise car-dependent city — a charming downtown, a riverwalk, a historic square. Useful if you live right at the core, but a car is still needed for most daily errands.

BloomingtonIN 6/10
The IU campus, Kirkwood Avenue, and downtown square are walkable and lively; the B-Line Trail adds cycling. Car-dependent beyond the core.
→
ChattanoogaTN 6/10
The riverfront and 13-mile Tennessee Riverwalk are walkable and beautiful, with free downtown CARTA shuttles. Walkable zones are high-quality and growing.
→
FayettevilleAR 6/10
Dickson Street and downtown are walkable; the 37-mile Razorback Greenway connecting to Bentonville is a genuine regional asset that supplements the core.
→
FlagstaffAZ 6/10
The historic Route 66 corridor and NAU campus are walkable, with a fare-free Mountain Line bus and the 50+ mile FUTS trail system — one of the best of any city here.
→
LexingtonKY 6/10
Chevy Chase and Woodland Park are walkable, with the Legacy and Town Branch trails for cycling. Above-average pedestrian infrastructure for a mid-sized Kentucky city.
→
NashuaNH 6/10
Main Street downtown is walkable and the Mine Falls Park trail system is excellent; the city is otherwise car-dependent. Boston 45 min supplements cultural needs.
→
Park CityUT 6/10
Walk Score 72 (Old Town 73); steep terrain limits walking but the free citywide transit and Main Street Trolley are exceptional for a resort town.
→
PittsburghPA 6/10
Shadyside, Squirrel Hill, Lawrenceville, and the South Side are walkable, with free downtown light rail and riverfront trails. Hilly geography limits walking outside the flat neighborhoods.
→
PrescottAZ 6/10
Courthouse Plaza and Whiskey Row are genuinely walkable and charming — a rarity in Arizona — with Granite Creek and Peavine trails nearby. Car-dependent beyond the Plaza.
→
Santa FeNM 6/10
The historic Plaza, Canyon Road, and the Railyard Arts District are walkable and culturally extraordinary, even if compact. Difficult Plaza parking actually encourages walking.
→
SarasotaFL 6/10
Downtown and St. Armands Circle are walkable and charming; most residential areas need a car. Above average for Florida, and car-optional near the core.
→
SpokaneWA 6/10
Kendall Yards and the Riverfront Park area are walkable, with the 37-mile Centennial Trail along the river. Improving infrastructure; Kendall Yards is a model new development.
→
TampaFL 6/10
Ybor City, Hyde Park, and the 2.5-mile Riverwalk are walkable, with a streetcar line. Better walkable zones than most big Florida cities, though the metro is car-oriented.
→
BozemanMT 5/10
Downtown Walk Score 89–94, citywide 47 — Main Street is surprisingly walkable but the fast-growing city beyond is entirely car-dependent. The contrast is sharp.
→
CarlsbadCA 5/10
Carlsbad Village is walkable with beach access, and the Coaster train reaches downtown San Diego in under an hour without a car. Suburban and car-dependent beyond the village.
→
Fort WorthTX 5/10
Sundance Square, Near Southside, and the West 7th area are walkable, with 100+ miles of Trinity Trails and TEXRail to DFW. Car-dependent between the walkable nodes.
→
GeorgetownTX 5/10
The historic courthouse Square — among the best-preserved in Texas — is walkable and charming, but a small zone in an otherwise sprawling suburban context.
→
KnoxvilleTN 5/10
Market Square and the Old City have urban energy, with the Tennessee Riverwalk and the 1,000-acre Urban Wilderness trail system. Car-dependent beyond downtown.
→
MadisonWI 5/10
The State Street pedestrian mall is among the best in the Midwest, and the isthmus between two lakes creates a compact, livable core. Suburban sprawl beyond.
→
MissoulaMT 5/10
Downtown Walk Score 74, citywide 45; the fare-free Mountain Line is a major asset for its size, with the Clark Fork River Trail and a strong cycling culture.
→
NaplesFL 5/10
Fifth Avenue South and Third Street South are walkable and beautiful with beach access on foot; beyond the small downtown, Naples is thoroughly car-dependent.
→
Palm SpringsCA 5/10
Palm Canyon Drive downtown is walkable; beyond it the city needs a car, and summer heat makes outdoor walking dangerous June–September. Walkable season is roughly October–May.
→
San AntonioTX 5/10
The River Walk, Pearl District, and King William are walkable; the River Walk is one of America's great urban pedestrian experiences, though more amenity than daily-life tool.
→
WilmingtonNC 5/10
The historic downtown and Cape Fear Riverwalk are walkable; beyond the core, the city and beaches are car-dependent. Decent downtown for a coastal NC city.
→

Tier 4

Car Required — Scores 3–4

A car is essential for daily life here. For many of these cities that is no surprise — but several score high on cost, healthcare, climate, or scenery, which makes walkability the conscious trade. A walkable downtown pocket may still exist; it simply does not cover daily errands.

ScottsdaleAZ 4/10
Old Town has limited walkability; everything else is car-dependent. The irony: one of the most expensive retirement destinations here, with among the worst walkability. The trade is Mayo Clinic and luxury, not pedestrian living.
→
RaleighNC 4/10
Downtown is improving and Fayetteville Street is walkable, with a 100+ mile greenway system — but rapid car-oriented growth means pedestrian infrastructure lags. Greenway is recreation, not daily-life walkability.
→
TucsonAZ 4/10
4th Avenue, the university area, and downtown are walkable, with a modern streetcar connecting them. Beyond the urban core, Tucson is car-dependent desert sprawl.
→
Hilton HeadSC 4/10
60+ miles of bike paths are a genuine asset for cyclists, but practical daily life requires a car. The resort lifestyle is built around driving between golf, beach, and dining.
→
Colorado SpringsCO 4/10
Old Colorado City is walkable; downtown limited. The Pikes Peak trail system is exceptional for recreation, but daily errands require driving across a sprawling layout.
→
SedonaAZ 4/10
Uptown is walkable for galleries and dining, but the town stretches miles along 89A — driving between areas is unavoidable. The red rock trail system is world-class for hiking, not errands.
→
DurangoCO 4/10
Downtown Walk Score 86, citywide 37 — the compact Victorian core is genuinely walkable with the Animas River Trail, but everything beyond downtown requires a car.
→
BoiseID 4/10
Downtown is walkable and the 25-mile Greenbelt along the Boise River is one of the Mountain West's finest urban greenways. Suburban and car-dependent beyond downtown.
→
Fort MyersFL 3/10
The River District downtown is walkable and charming, but a small oasis in a very car-dependent metro. The lack of walkability is a key tradeoff for its value and healthcare.
→
St. GeorgeUT 3/10
The lowest walkability score in the database — car-dependent even downtown. The trade is extraordinary: Zion, Snow Canyon, and Red Cliffs all within 30 minutes by car.
→
