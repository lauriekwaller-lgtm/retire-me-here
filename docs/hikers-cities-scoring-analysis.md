# RetireMeHere — Top Cities for Hikers (Scoring Analysis)

**Purpose:** Identify the strongest hiking-retirement cities from the curated 100-city RetireMeHere database, using a transparent and defensible methodology. This document is the source-of-truth scoring rubric and rationale, and serves as the standard for evaluating future additions or revisions to the Hikers landing page.

---

## Framing (applies to ALL landing pages)

> *"This ranking is drawn from RetireMeHere's curated database of U.S. retirement cities — a sampling of viable retirement destinations, not an exhaustive top-100. We add new cities regularly; if your favorite isn't here yet, it may be in a future update."*

This protects against "you missed [obvious city]" criticism while keeping the list honest about its scope.

---

## Scoring Methodology — Trail-Based Hiking Index

Three weighted dimensions, scored 0–10:

| Dimension | Weight | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Trail Access & Variety** | 40% | Proximity to substantial trail systems (within 30 min drive), miles of trail available, variety of difficulty (including gentle, paved, and ADA-accessible options for older hikers), terrain mix (forest, alpine, desert, canyon, river) |
| **Landscape Quality & Iconic Hikes** | 35% | Proximity to national parks, national monuments, designated wilderness, signature trails that anchor the area's identity (Zion, Grand Canyon, Smokies, Acadia, Glacier, etc.) |
| **Hiking Climate & Season Length** | 25% | How many months per year hiking is comfortable; whether summer heat or winter snow limits the season; altitude considerations |

**Why this framework is defensible:**
- Rewards cities where retired hikers can actually live and hike regularly, not just cities adjacent to famous hiking destinations
- Trail Access is weighted highest because daily hiking matters more to a retired hiker than the once-a-year iconic destination
- Climate matters but doesn't dominate — it informs which seasonal tier a city belongs in, not whether it makes the list at all
- Variety is rewarded over extremity — a city with 50 miles of varied trails outscores a city with one extreme trail
- Does NOT bias toward extreme terrain that retirees increasingly can't access. Trail Access explicitly rewards a strong gentle-hiking layer (paved interpretive trails, easy nature loops, ADA-accessible routes) alongside the dramatic stuff

**Retiree-specific calibration:**
- Mid-elevation, walkable trailheads are valued over distant high-altitude epic destinations
- Year-round knee-friendly access > seasonal peak experiences
- Reward cities with hiking community infrastructure (gear shops, group hikes, ranger-led programs) that serve solo retired hikers

**Affordability:**
Affordability is intentionally NOT a scoring dimension. The page is about hiking quality. Instead, the most expensive picks are surfaced in their own dedicated section ("For High-Budget Retirees") so readers can self-select honestly. This is a deliberate transparency choice — we honor world-class hiking landscapes even when they aren't financially accessible to most retirees, but we name the cost reality plainly.

---

## Page Structure — Seasonal Tiers + Budget Tier

Unlike the foodies page (which uses quality tiers), the hikers page is organized primarily by **when** you can hike — because for hikers, "what months can I actually use trails?" is the first practical question. A separate budget-tier section handles the highest-cost picks. Cities are scored on the rubric above, then placed in the appropriate tier:

- **Year-round hikeable** — Trails usable 10–12 months/year. Mild climate (winter trails accessible) and no extreme summer heat blocking summer hiking. Note: in higher-elevation year-round cities, winter traction devices may be needed for some trails.
- **Peak summer mountain towns** — High-altitude or northern cities. Trails are world-class May–October. Winter limits hiking significantly (snow, ice, closed roads).
- **Peak winter desert cities** — Mild winters make October–May the sweet spot. Summer heat forces dawn-only hiking June–September.
- **For high-budget retirees** — World-class hiking landscapes that require significant wealth to retire to (median home prices over $1.3M). Pulled into their own section for transparency.
- **Worth a look — strong regional alternatives** — Cities outside the dominant Mountain West region that offer real hiking access for retirees who want to stay east of the Rockies, in the Midwest, or near the coast.
