# RetireMeHere — Top Cities for LGBTQ+ Retirees (Scoring Analysis)

**Purpose:** Identify the strongest retirement cities for LGBTQ+ retirees from the curated RetireMeHere database, using a transparent and defensible methodology. This document is the source-of-truth scoring rubric and rationale, and serves as the standard for evaluating future additions or revisions to the LGBTQ+ Retirees landing page.

**Note on tone:** This rubric is written with more care than the other landing-page methodologies because the stakes for this audience are higher. LGBTQ+ retirees may be making decisions where the legal climate of a city or state affects medical decision-making, end-of-life rights, partner protections, and daily safety. This document errs toward caution and honesty over enthusiasm.

## What this list is measuring

The LGBTQ+ Retirees page is built around a specific question: *"Where can an LGBTQ+ retiree live openly, with community already in place, and legal protections that don't shift with each state legislative session?"*

That framing matters. LGBTQ+ retirement isn't just about welcoming neighborhoods — it's about a stable framework of legal protections (marriage rights, hospital visitation, healthcare directives, non-discrimination in housing and employment) combined with a visible community where the retiree won't be the only LGBTQ+ person in their building, their gym, their HOA, or their doctor's office.

**What this rubric measures:**
- **State-level legal protections** — comprehensive non-discrimination laws covering housing, employment, public accommodations; marriage equality protected at state level; healthcare directive recognition for same-sex partners
- **Municipal LGBTQ+ inclusion** — HRC Municipal Equality Index (MEI) score, local non-discrimination ordinances, transgender-inclusive municipal employee health benefits, LGBTQ+ liaison or community engagement
- **Visible LGBTQ+ community for retirees** — established community organizations, SAGE chapters or affiliates, pride events, LGBTQ+ retirement communities (Stonewall Gardens, Living Out, Village Hearth, Birds of a Feather, etc.), LGBTQ+-affirming places of worship, LGBTQ+-competent healthcare providers
- **Daily-life signal** — visible LGBTQ+ presence in everyday public life (not just one neighborhood, one bar, one weekend a year)

**What this rubric deliberately does NOT do:**
- Rank cities head-to-head on a single index — the criteria are too varied and the LGBTQ+ population's needs are too diverse
- Pretend that legal protections and community vibrancy are equivalent — they aren't. A welcoming neighborhood in a hostile state still leaves a retiree vulnerable in moments that matter (hospitalization, dispute over estate, etc.)
- Pretend that state laws are stable over a retiree's 20-30 year horizon — they aren't. The page links to lgbtmap.org/equality-maps so readers can check current status

## Scoring Methodology — Two-Tier Structural Filter

Unlike the other landing pages, this page does NOT use a weighted scoring index. The reason: legal protections aren't averageable with community vibrancy. A city with great community but no state protections is fundamentally different from a city with both — they're different products for different decisions, not points on the same scale.

Instead, the page uses a **two-tier structural filter**:

### Tier 1 — State + city both protect you

A city qualifies for Tier 1 if it meets **all three** criteria:

| Criterion | Definition | Source |
|---|---|---|
| **A. Strong state legal climate** | State has comprehensive non-discrimination law covering sexual orientation and gender identity in housing, employment, AND public accommodations. Marriage equality preserved at state level (not just federal). State has not recently passed legislation restricting LGBTQ+ rights. | Movement Advancement Project (MAP) equality maps (lgbtmap.org/equality-maps) — "high overall policy tally" |
| **B. Welcoming municipal climate** | HRC MEI score of 90+ OR local non-discrimination ordinance with LGBTQ+ coverage OR clear municipal record of LGBTQ+ inclusion (transgender-inclusive municipal employee benefits, LGBTQ+ liaison role, etc.) | HRC Municipal Equality Index annual reports |
| **C. Visible LGBTQ+ community** | At least one of: established SAGE chapter or affiliate, named LGBTQ+ retirement community in or near the city, annual Pride event of recognized scale, LGBTQ+ community center, recognition in established LGBTQ+ retirement guides (SAGE, Diverse Elders Coalition, Kiplinger LGBTQ+ retirement, RetireFabulously, etc.) | Multiple sources cross-checked |

**Why all three?** State law protects you in the medical, financial, and legal moments that affect retirement. Municipal climate makes daily life livable. Visible community is what keeps a retiree connected. Missing any of the three creates a meaningful tradeoff a retiree should know about before relocating.

### Tier 2 — Welcoming city, but state laws may not protect you

A city qualifies for Tier 2 if it meets **B and C but NOT A** — meaning:

- Welcoming municipal climate AND visible LGBTQ+ community
- BUT state lacks comprehensive non-discrimination protections, OR has passed legislation rolling back LGBTQ+ rights in recent years

Tier 2 honestly tells readers: *here are the welcoming cities, but check your specific legal needs against current state law before committing*. The page links to lgbtmap.org/equality-maps so readers can do that check.

**Why include Tier 2 at all?** Some retirees value local community more than state legal protections, especially if their specific needs (e.g., partner already resides there, family there, established medical providers) outweigh the state climate concerns. Others find their state laws less central to their daily lives than a welcoming local culture. Honoring this distinction is the most honest editorial choice.

**Why not a Tier 3?** A "welcoming but state-hostile" tier already addresses the practical retirees who would weigh local community heavily. Cities that lack both visible community AND state protections don't belong on this page in any tier — they're not viable LGBTQ+ retirement options regardless of how affordable or scenic they might otherwise be.

## Threshold Calibration — When State Climate Shifts

**State legal climates can shift, sometimes rapidly.** A few cases to flag:

- **Tier 1 → Tier 2 risk**: If a current Tier 1 state enacts legislation rolling back LGBTQ+ protections (e.g., overriding municipal non-discrimination laws, restricting gender-affirming care for adults, restricting same-sex marriage at state level), affected cities should move to Tier 2.
- **Tier 2 → Tier 1 possibility**: If a current Tier 2 state passes comprehensive non-discrimination legislation, affected cities can move to Tier 1.
- **Tier 2 → Drop**: If a Tier 2 city loses local protections AND state has actively hostile laws, the city should be removed.

**Recommended re-evaluation cadence:** Annually after the new HRC MEI release (typically November) and MAP equality map update.

## How to Evaluate a New City Against This Rubric

When a new city is added to the RetireMeHere database, use this checklist to determine LGBTQ+ landing page placement:

### Step 1 — State legal climate check
Look up the state on MAP equality maps (lgbtmap.org/equality-maps). Check the "overall policy tally" score.
- **High overall policy tally with no recent rollbacks** → state qualifies for Tier 1
- **Medium policy tally, or high tally but with recent restrictive legislation** → state pushes city to Tier 2 (regardless of municipal climate)
- **Low policy tally** → state pushes city to Tier 2 (only if municipal/community signals are strong)

### Step 2 — Municipal climate check
Look up the city on HRC's Municipal Equality Index (hrc.org/resources/mei-see-your-cities-scores). Check the most recent MEI score.
- **MEI 90-100** → strong municipal qualifier
- **MEI 70-89** → marginal; supplement with named LGBTQ+ inclusion signals (local non-discrimination ordinance, transgender-inclusive benefits)
- **MEI below 70 OR not rated** → city probably does not qualify for this page in any tier

### Step 3 — Visible community check
Verify at least one of:
- SAGE chapter or affiliate organization in the city
- Named LGBTQ+ retirement community in or near the city (within ~30 min)
- Annual Pride event of recognized scale (5,000+ attendees)
- LGBTQ+ community center
- Recognition in established LGBTQ+ retirement guides

If at least ONE of these is verified, the city has visible LGBTQ+ community presence.

### Step 4 — Tier assignment

| Tier | State (Step 1) | Municipal (Step 2) | Community (Step 3) |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Tier 1** | Strong | Strong | Verified |
| **Tier 2** | Medium or Low | Strong | Verified |
| **Do not include** | Any | Weak | Not verified |
| **Do not include** | Any | Strong | Not verified |
| **Do not include** | Strong | Weak | Verified |

The page deliberately excludes cities where ANY criterion is weak. A retiree relocating to a city specifically for LGBTQ+ retirement deserves a recommendation that meets a meaningful floor across all three areas.

## Considered but not included

This page does NOT use a "Considered but not included" section on the live page. The decision rationale:

For Foodies, Hikers, and Arts, that section names cities readers actively expect to see (Scottsdale, Savannah, etc.). On LGBTQ+ Retirees, naming a city as "not included" carries different weight — it could read as suggesting a city is LGBTQ+-unfriendly when the truth might be more nuanced (visible community present but municipal protections weak, etc.). The page already uses the Tier 2 framing to honor cities that are welcoming but legally complicated. There's no remaining set of cities that would benefit readers by being explicitly named as "considered but not included."

## Open Questions for Laurie's Review

1. **Page currently has 18 cities** (Tier 1: 12, Tier 2: 6). The page is shorter than other landing pages — this is intentional and appropriate. LGBTQ+ retirement requires a higher floor of confirmed signals than other lists. **The page should not be padded to match the others.**

2. **Pittsburgh and Alexandria are strong candidates for future addition.** Both meet Tier 1 standards based on documented HRC MEI scores, SAGE affiliates, and recognition in established LGBTQ+ retirement guides. Both should be added in the next page refresh.

3. **The rubric is designed to be applied to new database additions.** When you add new cities to your 100-city database (or replace existing entries), this rubric will let you assess LGBTQ+ landing page fit using the four-step process in "How to Evaluate a New City Against This Rubric."

4. **Annual re-evaluation cadence is critical.** State legal climates shift faster than other rubric criteria. Plan to re-check Tier 1 cities annually against MAP equality maps after the HRC MEI release each November. The intro-block reference to lgbtmap.org/equality-maps shifts some of that vigilance to the reader, but the page itself should not be left to drift.

5. **The intro-block is doing real editorial work** and should not be removed. It explains the state-vs-city distinction, names the legal stakes (marriage, hospital visitation, healthcare directives, discrimination), and points readers to external authority. This is the page's most important paragraph.
