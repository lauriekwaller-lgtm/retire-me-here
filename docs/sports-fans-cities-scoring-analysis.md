# RetireMeHere — Top Cities for Sports Fans (Scoring Analysis)

**Purpose:** Identify the strongest retirement cities for sports fans from the curated 100-city RetireMeHere database, using a transparent and defensible methodology. This document is the source-of-truth scoring rubric and rationale, and serves as the standard for evaluating future additions or revisions to the Sports Fans landing page.

## Scoring Methodology — Sports Access Index

The page is built on a single hard-edged threshold combined with a tier structure that captures different kinds of sports identity:

| Dimension | What it measures |
|---|---|
| **Pro Team Access (30-minute rule)** | Major-league teams (NFL, NBA, MLB, NHL, MLS, WNBA) whose home venue is within 30 minutes by car or rapid transit from the city center. This threshold reflects a real local team you can attend regularly, not a road-trip destination. |
| **Number of leagues represented** | Cities with teams in 4+ leagues form Tier 1 (Multi-sport metros). Cities with 2–3 teams form Tier 2 (Major sports cities). Cities with no pro teams but with elite college sports culture form Tier 3. |
| **Transit access counts equally with driving** | A city served by rapid transit to stadiums (Metro, light rail) qualifies on the same terms as a driving city. This is a retiree-specific calibration — many retirees prefer not to drive in metro traffic, and transit access is a real perk. |

**Why this framework is defensible:**
- The 30-minute threshold rules out "occasional road trip" scenarios. A retiree attending 10–20 games a year wants the games to be local, not a logistics project.
- Counting all five major leagues plus WNBA reflects the actual breadth of major-league sports in America today
- Multi-sport metros tier captures cities where you can follow multiple teams across multiple seasons
- The college tier honors a genuinely distinct retirement story — SEC and Big Ten towns where college football is the cultural calendar
- Counting Metro/transit access equally is the retiree-specific calibration that distinguishes this rubric from a generic "sports city" list. It surfaces Alexandria, VA, which has Metro access to the full DC sports portfolio in under 30 minutes.

**Retiree-specific calibration:**
- 30 minutes is the threshold because retirees value being able to attend games on a school night without it being an event
- WNBA included because women's basketball viewership has grown significantly and is a real consideration for many retirees
- College sports tier is included because for retirees from SEC, Big Ten, ACC, and similar conferences, college football Saturdays are often more central to their fan identity than any pro team
- Spring training is *not* included as its own dimension — it's a 6-week experience, not a year-round retirement consideration. Cities with both spring training and qualifying pro teams (Scottsdale, Tampa) get credit through their pro team counts.

**Why excluded from this list:**
NYC, Boston, Chicago, LA, Washington DC, San Francisco — these cities have unrivaled sports infrastructure but they are not in the RetireMeHere database because they aren't where most people consider retiring. Alexandria, VA captures DC sports access for retirees who want that experience without DC home prices.

## Page Structure — Three Tiers by Sports Identity

Unlike most other landing pages (which use quality tiers), the Sports Fans page is organized by **kind** of sports identity:

- **Multi-sport metros** — Cities with teams in 4 or more major leagues within 30 minutes
- **Major sports cities** — Cities with 2–3 major-league teams
- **Elite college sports towns** — Cities where college sports define the local sports culture

This structure rewards different audiences — the pro sports completionist, the regional pro fan, and the college sports loyalist — without falsely ranking one against another.

## Cities Considered — and Why Not Included

This page does NOT include a "Considered but not included" section on the live page. The decision rationale:

For Foodies, the section names cities readers actively expect to see (Scottsdale, Savannah, Sarasota). For Hikers, similar (Scottsdale, Santa Fe, Provincetown). On Sports Fans, no city in the database has a strong "why isn't this here?" case. Popular retirement cities like Charleston, Asheville, Naples, and Sarasota have no qualifying major-league teams — and that's not a surprise to readers searching for sports retirement cities. They already know.

If reader feedback later identifies omissions readers actively look for, a Considered section can be added.

**For internal reference, here are cities that were evaluated and didn't make the list:**

| City | State | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Annapolis | MD | Borderline 30-min to Northwest Stadium (Commanders) per research, but Baltimore and DC venues are 38-40 min away. Single-team access doesn't justify Tier 1 inclusion, and the Commanders connection is shaky on game-day traffic. Worth revisiting if the threshold loosens. |
| Henderson | NV | Has access to Raiders and Golden Knights similar to Las Vegas, but listing both Henderson and Las Vegas would duplicate the same sports access. Las Vegas chosen as the metro entry. |
| Georgetown | TX | Within 30 min of Austin FC (MLS only). Single-league access doesn't justify the page; Austin FC is the only qualifying team. |
| Carlsbad | CA | San Diego Padres and San Diego FC are 38-39 min away — past the 30-min threshold. |
| Sarasota | FL | Tampa Bay Rays at Tropicana Field is 44+ min — past threshold. |

| San Antonio | TX | Has Spurs (NBA) within 10 min — single team only. Same logic as Raleigh; honors the 2-team minimum for Tier 2. *Note: this is a real call worth revisiting if you want to expand Tier 2 to include 1-team cities.* |
