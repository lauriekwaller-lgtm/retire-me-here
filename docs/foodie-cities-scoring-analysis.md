**Purpose:** Identify the strongest culinary cities from the curated 100-city RetireMeHere database, using a transparent and defensible methodology. This document is the source-of-truth scoring rubric and rationale before building the live landing page.

---

## Scoring Methodology — Culinary Culture Index

Three weighted dimensions, scored 0–10:

| Dimension | Weight | What it measures |
|---|---|---|
| **Restaurant Scene Depth** | 40% | James Beard activity, Michelin presence, restaurant density, cuisine diversity, independent vs. chain ratio |
| **Local Food Culture** | 35% | Year-round farmers markets, food halls, public markets, locally-sourced restaurant culture, specialty food retail, food festivals |
| **Culinary Identity** | 25% | Signature food story, cookbook authors / food media / culinary schools rooted there, multicultural depth, regional cuisine traditions |

**Why this framework is defensible:**
- Doesn't bias toward NYC/SF/Chicago (which retirees rarely move to anyway)
- Rewards cities with markets-and-makers cultures, not just restaurant counts
- Surfaces affordable retirement-realistic cities while still rewarding genuine quality
- Gives unique credit to cities with rooted culinary identity (Tucson, Santa Fe, Pittsburgh)

**Key external credentials honored in scoring:**
- **UNESCO Cities of Gastronomy** — only 2 in the US: Tucson (2015) and San Antonio (2017). Both in your database. Major credential.
- **James Beard Foundation Awards** — semifinalist/finalist/winner activity is the gold standard for restaurant prestige
- **Michelin presence** — limited but factored where applicable (e.g., Napa)

## Final 15 Cities — Ranked

### Tier 1 — Dining Destinations (8.0+) | 5 cities

These are the "world knows about them" food cities. Visitors plan trips around restaurants here. Retirees living here have year-round access to nationally-recognized dining.

| Rank | City | State | Score | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **New Orleans** | LA | **9.5** | Most distinctive culinary identity in America. Creole + Cajun heritage, multi-generational restaurant dynasties (Brennan's, Commander's Palace), French Quarter, Crescent City Farmers Market, music-and-food fusion. Multiple JBF wins/year. |
| 2 | **Charleston** | SC | **9.4** | Husk, FIG, McCrady's legacy. Sean Brock revived Lowcountry/Gullah cuisine globally. Charleston Wine + Food festival. Multiple JBF wins/year. Walkable historic district packed with destination restaurants. |
| 3 | **Portland** | ME | **9.0** | Bon Appétit "Restaurant City of the Year" (2018). Eventide Oyster, Fore Street, Drifter's Wife. Tight, walkable food scene. Strong seafood identity, French/Spanish technique. |
| 4 | **Philadelphia** | PA | **8.7** | Reading Terminal Market (legendary). Italian Market (oldest continuously-operating outdoor market in US). Multiple JBF chefs annually. Cheesesteak identity. Increasingly diverse: Vietnamese, Cambodian, modern American. |
| 5 | **Tucson** | AZ | **8.6** | **UNESCO City of Gastronomy (first in US, 2015).** 4,000+ year agricultural history. Sonoran cuisine identity. Heritage crops (tepary beans, mesquite, chiles). Mexican food capital of America. 24+ annual food festivals. |

### Tier 2 — Strong Food Culture (7.5–7.9) | 5 cities

Real food cities, but more locally-known than nationally famous. Retirees here will find consistent quality and culinary depth.

| Rank | City | State | Score | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 6 | **Asheville** | NC | **7.9** | Cúrate, Rhubarb, multiple JBF semifinalists. WNC Farmers Market. French Broad Chocolate. Strong farm-to-table identity. Beer-and-food scene. Mountain food culture. |
| 7 | **Madison** | WI | **7.8** | Dane County Farmers Market — **largest producer-only farmers market in the US**. L'Etoile (Tory Miller, James Beard winner). Wisconsin cheese + supper club traditions. Strong locally-sourced dining culture. |
| 8 | **Napa** | CA | **7.7** | Wine country dining. Adjacent to French Laundry / Bouchon (Yountville). Oxbow Public Market. Michelin presence. More "destination" than daily-living food, but exceptional quality. |
| 9 | **Santa Fe** | NM | **7.7** | Green chile signature. Santa Fe Farmers Market consistently ranked top in US. Geronimo, Sazon, Santacafé. Multiple JBF winners. Distinctive regional cuisine identity. |
| 10 | **Nashville** | TN | **7.5** | Hot chicken capital. Multiple JBF chefs (Husk Nashville, Bastion, Lockeland Table, Margot Café). Country music + food culture. Growing fine dining scene alongside meat-and-three traditions. |

### Tier 3 — Notable Food Culture (7.0–7.4) | 5 cities

Distinctive culinary identities, often surprising picks. Strong reasons to retire here for food, but more niche.

| Rank | City | State | Score | Why |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 11 | **San Antonio** | TX | **7.4** | **UNESCO City of Gastronomy (2017).** Pearl District (curated food + farmers market hub). Tex-Mex / Mexican heritage. Breakfast taco capital. Mission-trail cuisine. |
| 12 | **Pittsburgh** | PA | **7.3** | Strip District (legendary public market). Eastern European heritage (pierogi, kielbasa). Modern renaissance — multiple JBF semifinalists. Sandwich identity (Primanti's). Affordable for serious food city. |
| 13 | **Burlington** | VT | **7.2** | Hen of the Wood, Honey Road (JBF). Vermont farm-to-table pioneer. Church Street Marketplace. Vermont Cheese Trail. Strong farmers market and ECHO Center for sustainable food culture. |
| 14 | **Bentonville** | AR | **7.1** | Walmart-funded culinary investment. Coler Cuisine (JBF semifinalist), The Hive, The Preacher's Son. Bentonville Farmers Market. Adjacent to Crystal Bridges arts ecosystem. Underrated dark horse. |
| 15 | **Memphis** | TN | **7.0** | BBQ identity (legitimately world-class). Soulsville / soul food heritage. Crosstown Concourse food hall. Memphis Farmers Market. Stronger on identity than breadth. |
